I did not fully understand how complicated the tool had become until the interface appeared on my screen.

What I originally wanted was simple: let an AI agent observe my everyday Codex records, extract topics worth developing, and place them in a list. I would choose one, send it to HomePage, keep editing it there, and decide later whether to publish it.

Along the way, that small tool accumulated scoring, multiple approval stages, revisions, hash chains, preview packages, and a dedicated category. Each addition had a reasonable explanation. Content quality needed protection. Accidental publishing had to be prevented. Changes should be traceable. Private material had to stay separate from public writing. The logic made sense, and the engineering was moving quickly.

The problem was that I wanted to use it less and less.

## I could not see the complexity until the interface appeared

Complexity is quiet when it lives in code. It hides in files, fields, and workflows. Before those parts become something I have to operate, it is difficult to feel how much attention they will consume.

The interface turned every hidden rule into an action I had to understand. I was no longer merely told that the system would preserve a review history. I had to work out which state an article was in, what I was supposed to click next, and how one approval differed from another. A tool meant to save my energy now required me to learn its internal logic before I could use it.

Only then did I realize that the friction did not come from any single button. It came from needing to understand a new layer of language at every step. The system might have been rigorous as engineering, but as a product it had become a wall.

We eventually cut it back to a topic list. Each item now says only what the topic is, which angle it could take, why it is worth writing, and what source material is available. My decision is simple: send it to HomePage or ignore it.

There was less information, yet the meaning was clearer. For the first time, I had a plain reaction: I can use this. Not because the tool did more, but because my mind could finally process what was in front of me.

## Why I did not stop it sooner

Looking back, the AI agent did not make the system complicated in secret. If anything, it explained almost every choice.

It presented plans, reasons, risks, alternatives, and verification steps. Taken one section at a time, the logic may not have been wrong. But the responses were long and layered. I did not have enough energy to absorb that much text at once, and I also wanted the project to keep moving. The easiest answers became “Agreed,” “Continue,” and “Proceed with the recommended approach.”

Those answers looked like decisions. Often, they were simply the point at which I stopped reading.

Formally, I still held the power to approve. The AI did not bypass me or publish anything on its own. But when understanding a proposal requires sustained attention and saying “continue” takes only a few seconds, the choice begins to lose its meaning. I remained inside the process without truly participating in it.

That state becomes harder to notice with repeated use. AI output tends to look complete, professional, and carefully reasoned. At first I read it closely. Later I skimmed it. Eventually I only wanted to know whether the work could move forward. I was not unaware that the agent might be overdesigning the system. I had simply become numb to the weight of its explanations, until even objecting felt like work.

## When complexity takes away the right to say no

People often say that important decisions should keep a human in the loop. But placing a person in front of an approval button does not mean that person still has control.

When an AI hands over all of its internal reasoning, engineering details, and risk analysis at once, it may appear to be fully transparent. In practice, it can push the entire cost of judgment back onto the human. Once the information exceeds what someone can process in the moment, informed consent decays into mechanical consent.

A good agent should not use more words to prove how thoroughly it has thought. It should compress complexity first: what decision is needed now, what the main cost is, and which path it recommends. The underlying detail can remain available when the human wants to inspect it. Execution logic the agent can safely handle should not become another workflow the user has to learn.

I used to think that making content production more efficient meant asking an AI agent to do more. Now I would use a different test: does it make it easier for me to make a real decision?

The next time I feel ready to click “Continue” immediately, I should probably pause and ask: do I actually agree, or am I simply too tired to keep reading?
